Four Tips to Identify Inactive Customers

Stefan Koenig on Oct 12, 2015

Inactive customers are a fact of life for marketers. An avid reader of the Chicago Tribune may let her subscription lapse when she moves to Philadelphia. A couple who spent hundreds of dollars a month at Home Depot may drop back to only $100 a year once their home remodel is complete. And a Toys “R” Us Rewards “R” Us loyalty club member may disengage after her children reach a certain age.

Often, customer inactivity has nothing to do with poor marketing or product offerings – disengagement can simply be a matter of a major life change or even something as simple as switching to a new email. However, continuing outreach to these inactive customers is a waste of marketing resources, and could actually turn a positive brand experience negative.

As we’ve discussed previously, consumers expect companies to know them intimately, and that means knowing when they are no longer interested in receiving your messages. Continuing to market to an inactive customer indicates to them that you don’t understand their needs, and may make them less likely to think of you or recommend you in the future.

In some cases, these contacts can be re-engaged through the right promotions, personalized messages and product offerings (stay tuned as we examine re-engagement strategies in a later post), but first, it’s essential to find out just who these customers are. Here are four ways marketers can identify inactive customers.

1) Identify people who haven’t engaged over a certain period of time. Mining your list for people who haven’t purchased an item, opened or clicked an email, logged into your app, etc., in a given time frame is the simplest and most common method for identifying inactive customers. The specific time frame will depend on your individual company’s products, services and communication frequency. A beverage company, for example, has a much shorter sales cycle and often higher communication frequency than a luxury car manufacturer, so this kind of company may consider a person inactive after 2-3 months vs. a year or more for the car maker. No matter what the timeframe is, it’s important to continually look for unengaged customers and segment your marketing efforts accordingly.

2) Determine your typical purchase frequency, and look for outliers. If you know that your typical consumer makes a purchase at least once every three months, segment your customer database to look for contacts who haven’t been meeting this average. While some disparity in frequency is likely, contacts who consistently don’t meet your average frequency or order size should be treated differently than those who do.

3) Look for sudden changes. Once you have a strong understanding of your individual customer’s identities, it will be easy to flag discrepancies. Does a customer who previously purchased weekly now only purchase once a month? Did a customer who opened every single email you sent suddenly stop? These changes are frequent indicators of changing needs, lifestyles and habits. Customers exhibiting these signs should be communicated to strategically before they fully disengage.

4) Find patterns. Once you’ve identified a set of inactive customers – either because they haven’t engaged in a certain time frame, don’t meet purchase averages or have changed their behaviors, look for commonalities among these contacts so that you can better refine your marketing efforts going forward. Did these customers all come from an online ad offering a certain promotion? Perhaps those are one off customers who will only return for a very good deal. Did they all buy a certain product? Perhaps you should try cross-promoting different products, or reach out to see if they had a poor experience.

Understanding your inactive customers is a critical step in creating 360° customer profiles and determining effective marketing strategies. Finding out why customers become inactive can help you better tailor your messages, product offerings and promotional strategies to maximize your marketing ROI, and stop wasting your energy on people who are unlikely to make a purchase.
